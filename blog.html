From a Personal Challenge to 1st Place — My URC Research Journey

I made a commitment to myself at the start of this semester: to step outside my comfort zone and join a competition — the Undergraduate Research Conference at Brigham Young University–Hawaii. I didn't know exactly where it would lead, but I knew I had to try.

At first, it felt daunting. I was balancing three major classes that demanded significant time and focus. Still, I felt both nervous and excited — and I decided to take the leap. I'm deeply grateful to my mentor, Dr. Michael Morris, Ph.D., who helped me turn a broad interest into a focused research question. His guidance — from using AI tools to introducing me to Zotero, and encouraging me to strengthen my methodology — played a huge role in shaping this work.

I also want to recognize my husband, who stepped up in the most meaningful way. He took care of our baby most of the time so I could focus on research. I couldn't have done this without his support. This journey took around 60–72 hours of reading articles, reviewing journals and policies, and writing drafts. In the middle of it all, the Kona Storm hit, the power went out, and the deadline was approaching fast. I honestly thought I wouldn't make it. But I kept going.

Key Takeaway
"With grit, focus, and the willingness to try, we're capable of far more than we think. What started as a personal challenge turned into something much bigger — 1st Place in the Business Research Award."

Spring 2026 Marketing Reflection — BUSM 304

The Cola Wars Taught Me More About Marketing Than I Expected

This week in my marketing class at BYUH, I watched the History Channel's Cola Wars documentary — and it completely changed how I think about everyday purchasing decisions. Two nearly identical products locked in a billion-dollar battle. The real advantage? Understanding the consumer.

Our class recreated the famous Pepsi Challenge blind taste test. Before the test, as a proud Coca-Cola fan, my answer was easy: Coke, no hesitation. Then came the real challenge — could I actually tell the difference without seeing the label? I could. And apparently so could most of my class: the final score came out 14 votes for Coca-Cola and 10 for Pepsi. Coke won our classroom Cola War!

But this is where it gets interesting from a marketing perspective. This exercise clearly showed the consumer decision-making process from Chapter 5. Even in a blind test, our brand loyalty, emotions, and perceptions influenced how we tasted and judged each drink. We weren't just evaluating flavor — we were evaluating everything we associate with that brand. Pepsi succeeded in market segmentation by targeting younger consumers with bold celebrity endorsements like Michael Jackson. Meanwhile, Coca-Cola focused on tradition and emotional storytelling. Two brands, two very different positioning strategies — but the same brown fizzy drink.

Key Takeaway
"Great marketing isn't just about the product — it's about the relationship you build with your target market. Emotions, culture, and identity influence purchase decisions much more than features or even taste."

Spring 2026 Marketing Research — BUSM 304

Real Data. Real Insights. Real Marketing.

This week in my Principles of Marketing class at BYU–Hawaii, I simultaneously read Chapters 3 and 4 of the Kotler textbook and completed a comprehensive secondary data analysis assignment in Excel. Recognizing that both activities conveyed the same core concepts significantly deepened my understanding.

I analyzed a dataset of 295 customers, examining their incomes, spending habits, and demographic characteristics. The data revealed: the average customer age is 49.8 years — the largest age group (35.6%) is 49–54 years old. Average income is $116,112, ranging from $50,125 to $179,955. Average monthly expenditure is $203.67. Customers earning over $100,000 rated the brand marginally higher (3.1 out of 5) than the overall average of 3.0. Male customers (56.3% of the sample) rated the brand slightly higher (3.1) vs. female customers (2.9).

To generate these insights, I built pivot tables using COUNTIF and COUNTIFS formulas, created pie charts for gender and marital status, developed a horizontal bar chart for education, and grouped customers into five age bands. Chapter 4 asserts that marketers should integrate traditional research with digital tools — this assignment gave me hands-on practice doing exactly that.

Key Takeaway
"Marketing isn't guesswork — it's patterns hiding in data, waiting to be found."

Spring 2026 Marketing Strategy — BUSM 304

Being Big Isn't Always Best — Being Efficient Is What Really Counts

This week in my Principles of Marketing class at BYU–Hawaii, I examined company and marketing strategy — and one assignment completely changed how I see the numbers behind big brands. I analyzed Facebook vs. Google using key metrics like marketing ROI, net marketing contribution, and marketing ROS — and discovered that Facebook is actually the more efficient marketer, despite Google having twice the sales!

One of my biggest takeaways from Chapter 2 was learning how companies use the product/market development grid to grow in smart ways — whether through market penetration, market development, product development, or diversification. It's not only about more sales; it's about making thoughtful choices about where and how to grow. Market segmentation and targeting allow companies to focus on their most valuable customer groups rather than trying to serve everyone. This focused strategy drives profitability — not just revenue.

Key Takeaway
"Whether you're reading a BCG growth-share matrix or figuring out your marketing mix, the lesson is the same: data plus strategy equals smarter marketing."

Spring 2026 Everyday Marketing — BUSM 304

What I Learned About Marketing on a Bus Ride

This week, on my way home from my internship on TheBus in Oahu, I noticed something about marketing. I took my seat and saw the entire ceiling covered with local business ads. At first, they just looked like decorations. Looking more closely, I realized there was a plan behind each ad's placement.

I started to see ideas from class in everyday life. Using what I learned from Chapter 1 of Kotler and Armstrong's Principles of Marketing, I saw that each ceiling ad was a market offering — a mix of products, services, and experiences meant to meet a need or want. The brands weren't putting up ads at random. They targeted the needs, wants, and demands of daily bus riders in Oahu. Needs are basic things people feel they lack. Wants are shaped by culture and temperament. When people can pay for what they want, those wants become demands.

The best ads on TheBus didn't list features. They spoke to what commuters actually needed. My boring commute became a real marketing lesson — and a reminder to look for marketing in action wherever I go.

Key Takeaway
"The lessons from class go beyond theory — they shape how businesses and people interact in everyday life. Marketing is everywhere, if you train yourself to see it."
